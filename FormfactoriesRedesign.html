Overview

Project Type

This was both a student class project and a client project for a website redesign. The client runs a small startup 3D-printing company, Formfactories.

Objective

To make a high-fidelity mockup of the client’s company website for Formfactories that will effectively market his 3D-printing business and services towards potential customers.

Duration

10 weeks

My Role

I contributed to most, if not all of the tasks needed for the website redesign. This includes the following:

  • User Research - Interviews, Competitive Analysis
  • Data Synthesis - Personas, User Scenarios, and Use Cases
  • Prototyping - Creative Brief, Information Architecture, Sketches, Wireframing
  • Iteration - User Testing, Design Requirements and Feedback Implementation

Tools

Google Suite, Figma

Core Team

Alexander Chen, Denise Tang, Lixiang (Janice) Zhou

Mentors

David Kirsh - He is a professor within the Cognitive Science department at University of California - San Diego. He taught the course in which this website was revolved around (Practicum in Pro Web Design), helped to teach the fundamentals of working with clients, and give constructive critique on the progress of our team’s website.

Adam Mekrut, Trevor Elwell, and Emily Nguyen - These were the TAs that facilitated the course. They gave insights, guidance, and critique to our group’s progress.

Design Challenge

Problem Background

Within our team, Alexander was able to connect us with a client that needed a website. Our client, Michael Armbruster, the owner of Incept 3D, wanted a website that would automate the process of 3D-printing orders for his partner company, Formfactories.

Solution

We designed a high-fidelity mockup of Formfactories’ website on both mobile and desktop to incorporate aspects of the ordering process of obtaining a 3D-printed part, keeping track of orders, and getting information about the 3D printers and materials that Formfactories hosts.

Research

Interviews

We interviewed with Michael to pinpoint his needs and understandings of his potential website. We also asked people who were familiar with 3D-printing on what their needs were with 3D-printing. Below are highlights from some of the notes gathered that informed us of prioritizing specific features of the website.

Client’s Perspective

Purpose of the business

Formfactories is a 3D printing company and serves the middle market. Formfactories produces quick prototypes in plastic. The goal of Formfactories is to have as much automation as possible. Customers place orders through Formfactories, Formfactories prints the file and delivers the file to the client.

Purpose of Formfactories’s Website

Be as prestigious as any Silicon Valley tech company, but function like Amazon where the customer gets to do everything to get their 3D printing part.

Company Brand

Have a “minimalist feel”, quick, snappy, professional, and techy.

Types of People that Use the Service

Majority of customers, 90% of them, are mechanical engineers, but there are niche industries that find out about 3D printing.

How Formfactories compares to Competitors

Has direct manufacturing with quick parts versus other places where they outsource their 3D printing. More transparent in its actual capabilities for 3D printing. However, other competitors claim they have a comprehensive list of 3D printing options and services to choose from.

Website Content

Mostly just rewording the way they represent their products, 3D printers, printer specifications, and 3D printing material.

Other People’s Perspectives

Strong Considerations When 3D Printing a Part

  • Ability to upload CAD file and get an instant quote with a time estimate of completing the order for the part.
  • Control over setting up 3D printed part to make changes if needed.
  • Financially expensive to outsource.
  • Quality assurance and control.
  • Business knows what their doing to gain trust. Impression of being knowledgeable about their business and what they do.
  • Different factors to control, such as size of print, how detailed it is, how complex, supports and brims, etc.
  • Have someone print the design for me.
  • Materials and their usage.
  • How dimensionally accurate the print is.
  • Ability to print the part with the provided machines.

Competitive Analysis

We also researched some potential competitors of Incept3D and Formfactories, specifically looking into the websites of other 3D printing companies. We analyzed what notable features they had based on their branding, website functionality, content, site architecture, site navigation, and visual design that could inspire features to incorporate within our redesign of Formfactories’ website.

Data Synthesis

From our competitive analysis, we analyzed what notable features these companies had that could inspire features to incorporate within the website, within each category (branding, functionality, content, etc.) and in general for all of our competitors.

We had key takeaways and a few ideas that would help inform us of the potential features that the redesigned website could include.

Takeaways

Some notable factors that the competitors' websites included were lead-times for finishing the 3D-printed part, cost of their services, efficiency of their 3D printing process, ability to customize 3D printed parts, how easy-to-use their service is, and an adaptive user flow that conveniently and efficiently walks the person through ordering their 3D printed part(s).

The overall appearance of a website can exude many different forms of professionalism and communication of 3D printed services. This would be dependent upon what specific needs and services Formfactories can provide.

There must be a balance between prompting the person to get a quote on their orders and showcasing information about Formfactories services so that the person can easily access relevant information that will ease their use of Formfactories's 3D printing service.

Ideas from Competitors' Websites to Include

Action-oriented buttons to encourage users to submit their files clearly visible on every page.

Include clear photos/graphics when listing the various 3D printing techniques to help the user decide which is right for them.

Instant quote and estimated delivery date are important to include upfront to address people's primary concerns about the costs and waiting times for Formfactories's services.

More novel features, such as recommendations for material usage to a CAD file can differentiate Formfactories's from its competitors.

Personas, User Scenarios, and Use Cases

We summarized our findings and understandings of Formfactories' typical stakeholders into 3 different personas, each with their set of circumstances and typical tasks that they would need to do on a website. This was to illustrate their background and needs in relation to 3D printing.

Persona - Steve

Steve needs to conscientiously, but efficiently, make reliable prototypes of expected quality to conplete stringent deadlines.

User Scenarios and Use Cases - Steve

Steve typically needs quality prints as well as bulk orders of parts. Below are some of the tasks that he would need to know and do when using a 3D-printing service online.

Persona - Jonathan

Jonathan, another persona, prefers quality and control over his parts to suit his personal projects, and would like to know if any businesses that he outsource his parts to can ensure parts are properly made.

User Scenarios and Use Cases - Jonathan

Jonathan probably needs his part(s) made that he can't do on his own with the current 3D-printing capabilities that he has. These are his tasks that he needs to know an do when considering a 3D-printing service online.

Persona - Sandra

Sandra, another persona, needs complex parts to be produced quickly and cheaply for the constant lab projects and tests that she needs to run.

User Scenarios and Use Cases - Sandra

Sandra quickly needs parts that are custom-made, have specific materials that she cannot easily print, or of both qualities. These are some of the things she has to go through to use a 3D-printing service online.

Ideation

Based on our primary and secondary research, our team sketched out different layouts of the potential webpages that the Formfactories' websites would have. We had rough ideas on the general content of the landing page, the organization of different 3D printers and materials shown, and the walkthrough of the ordering process for 3D-printed parts.

Information Architecture

I helped to visualize what pages were needed for the website and how these pages are related to one another through making a rough draft of the information architecture. This was to help the team prioritize on what webpages to develop that would make Formfactories to be informative, transparent, and efficient about its services. This information architecture was iterated upon later once we fulfilled making the main wireframes of the main sections of the initial site architecture.

Moodboard

We had a moodboard to inform us of the visual design and branding of the website to make it techy, simple, and professional, as the client preferred.

A group consensus would be reached to use a blue monochrome color palette, though no formal visual style guide has been established in the iterations.

Prototyping

Our earlier prototypes referred to the earlier information architecture. The wireframes are in a landscape orientation and assume to take up the resolution of a conventional computer instead of a phone because of how the industry work done for manufacturing and 3D-printing parts mainly come from computer-based, CAD software. Conventional, industry-standard software, like SolidWorks and Autodesk Inventor, that produces files compatible for 3D-printers are widely available and used on computers, and not necessarily on phones. Below are the main wireframes that I had developed.

Comparison of 3D Printers

Because Formfactories only supports printers that can do stereolithography (SLA) or fusion deposition modeling (FDM), there would a simple side-by-side comparison between the available materials, resolutions, potential applications, and other factors that each printer supports.

Available Materials and Characteristics

Other than 3D-printing capabilities, available materials and different material types would be shown (represented as PLA and Standard Resin). Included are benefits and drawbacks for using each material type.

Customizing Printer Settings

After someone selects which 3D printer to use (SLA or FDM), they would be able to upload their CAD file of the desired printed part(s) and adjust the color, material, and other settings of their part. An estimated price would be automatically calculated and shown based on the current settings shown for the person.

Completing the Order

To ensure delivery of the part, the person would input their billing address, shipping address, desired payment option, and production speed of the ordered parts. Along with the final price, an estimated time of delivery would be shown.

User Testing

Approach

The team asked two people about their knowledge in 3D printing and what their most important considerations were for successfully obtaining their 3D printed part(s).

We had participants interact with the prototype so that we could observe and record how the participants uploaded CAD files and submitted orders for their 3D printed part, how the participants learned more about the types of 3D printers and materials that were available, and how the participants searched for answers to questions that they had in mind about Formfactories’s services.

Finally, we asked the participants follow-up questions related to how they engaged with these tasks and any feedback that they wanted to share to inform changes to the website.

Findings

What participants liked about our website were that there were design considerations that helped to provide applicable use for materials, yet there was a need to clarify what the post-processing service of 3D printed parts.

Participants considered cost, speed, and reliability as important considerations for their willingness to use Formfactories’s services.

Participants expressed a preference to be able to keep track of their history of orders and payment information that they had.

There was general confusion on some of the specific text on the website. One person was confused about what "post-processing" means as a service during the ordering process, another mentioned that there should be a clear difference between lead time and shipping time.

Iterations

Background of the First Iterations

Despite initial successes with drafting out the features derived from user research, consolidated design artifacts, and the information architecture, findings from user testing and an initial examination of the wireframes informed issues with the features that needed to be fixed. Many of the changes that followed for the first iteration also followed an expanded version of the information architecture for the website.

Below are the changes made to some of the features and wireframes of the initial prototype of the redesign.

Ordering Process

Problems with the Initial Design

Despite how the initial wireframes tried to streamline the ordering process through smaller and manageable sequential tasks, there was a need to improve the consistency and clarity of the overall layout and content of the wireframes that show the ordering process.

The ordering process has an interface that is styled very differently from other sections of the Formfactories website, and the lack of navigation bar reduces accessibility for the person to navigate away from the ordering process and into other sections of the website. This would seem as if the ordering process is disconnected and largely removed from the website itself.

The available space on the interface on each section of the ordering process seemed small and the interface had a vague, large background image with no real purpose or use behind it.

Finally, the indication of the steps within the ordering process doesn’t effectively explain the tasks the person has to do before finalizing their order of their 3D-printed part.

Changes Made to the Feature

To improve the consistency and layout, the re-addition of the navigation bar and greater usage of space of the custom settings was a start. This helped bring greater visibility and accessibility to both user interface components for custom 3D-printing settings and to navigation links.

For greater clarity to the different steps of the ordering process, a step progress bar with labels grouped to each circle was redesigned to provide enough information for the person to more intuitively understand what they need to do to fulfill their order for their part(s).

A description is added to the asterisk symbol to inform the person of default settings applied and another description explains the pricing and definition of the post-processing service.

Information about 3D Printers and Materials

Problems with the Initial Design

The first wireframes to help people learn more about the types of 3D printers and materials tried to incorporate a two-column grid that would easily compare 3D printers and materials side by side. However, when comparing between the 3D printing types, many pieces of information seemed to be scattered along a single page, which made materials and design recommendations inconveniently located towards the bottom of the page and much of the information for 3D printing types very dense and disorganized.

Changes Made to the Feature

Improved typography and use of negative space helped to organize text that corresponded to each type of 3D printer, in which the configuration of a grid-like table would emerge. An added sticky header to label the types of 3D printing would remain at the top of the page when the person scrolls down to compare the printers, making it easier to reference the descriptions and differences of each printer to each associated factor (i.e. applications, leadtime, resolution, infill).

An anchor link underlined and labeled “Materials and Recommendations” would be visible at the top of the page, which would consolidate information of all material types and their design recommendations into a pop-up modal window. Within the modal window, a tab ribbon between FDM and SLA makes it convenient for the person to switch viewing materials from material types of SLA to FDM and vice versa. People would be able to scroll down to reveal more information of all other materials and view design recommendations.

Additional Features Added

From individuals during user testing, there was an expressed need to incorporate a history of previous orders and payments. The addition of a login system and account creation would help catalogue and manage the person's orders.

"Final" Handoff

In the end, there was no strong follow-up with the client and this version of the redesign was handed off to the professor and mentors for final feedback. Our group never had the chance to meet with our client again. This was the second iteration that the group ended on before our group went our separate ways. More Changes Made

Several months after the second iteration of the website was handed off, I independently re-reviewed the user research, personas, user scenarios, use cases, and user testing. Based on this review, I changed the information shown on different pages of the ordering process and on the modal window for materials and design recommendations.

Ordering Process - Customizing the 3D Printed Part(s)

Reasons for Changes

When the person goes to make any custom changes to 3D printer settings on their part(s), there would be labels indicating the dimensions of the part(s) and technical details of the printer settings. There was consideration from more than one user scenarios of our personas when a person may need to mass produce more than one part as well as incorporating user feedback about adding more helpful hints from the FAQ page to the ordering process.

Although the post-processing service has a description outlining the benefits of using the service and its cost, the different labels for the dimensions of the part(s) and technical details of the printer settings may not effectively clarify how they affect the printed part. The post-processing service also doesn't explain how it affects the speed of the delivery of the part(s).

Before

Changes Made

Rewording the dimensions and adding descriptions to the labels were needed to iterate upon these discovered issues. For the dimensions, "Length", "Width", and "Height" would better ensure that the printed part(s) would adhere to the desired size that the person expects from the part(s), rather than a vague lettering system for labels ("X", "Y", "Z").

Quantity of parts, as an additional custom setting, would afford the person to order multiple quantities of the same part. since it would be the case for people to need to order multiple copies of a printed part for prototyping and testing purposes (as outlined from user scenarios). The substitution of the material color of the part for quantity of parts to order is only to visually represent the functionality for ordering multiple parts. The potential list of printer settings shown for the wireframes is not exhaustive.

Since speed of completion for 3D printed parts was evidenced from prior user research and use scenarios, I added "+ 2 hours" to clarify how long the post-processing service would potentially take as well as an estimated time for delivery. Similar to the estimated price shown, the estimated delivery time would dynamically change based on how the person would customize their printer settings.

After

Ordering Process - Confirming Order for Parts

Reasons for Changes

The second iteration of the confirmation page effectively showed a lot of relevant information organized in a scannable manner, but there was essential information that was missing on this page. For instance, other than "Edit" buttons that vaguely describe the person to make changes, nothing else is shown to inform the person of what to do during this confirmation page. Ironically, even though the estimated price and delivery time is shown when the person makes changes to printer settings of their custom part(s), this information is also not shown when the person needs to review every aspect of their order. This could result in the person needing to navigate back through other sections of the ordering process to remember how much their quote and estimated delivery time.

Before

Changes Made

"Confirm your Order?" would be an appropriate title shown to succinctly illustrate that the person is approaching their final step of ordering their 3D-printed part(s). An added description below the title clarifies exactly what the person should do by informing the person to carefully and deliberately review the information shown before proceeding to finalize their decision on their order. Changes to wording of the buttons below each section (from "Edit" to "Make Changes") as well as the buttons' appearance make the buttons more recognizable to the person to make those necessary changes. Information that was changed from the previous step of customizing the person's 3D-printed part has been reflected in the confirmation page. The distinction between the cost of the post-processing service and the price of printing the part(s) is also made apparent in the third section regarding payment and delivery information.

After

Information Shown for Materials and Design Recommendations

Reasons for Changes

The second iteration of presenting information of the materials and design recommendations for SLA and FDM printers had limited amount of space to view within the modal window, which reduces the accessibility of showing design recommendations since the design recommendations were placed all the way at the bottom of the list of materials shown. There was a lack of incorporation and detail of the design recommendations and how to make them scannable to quickly read them.

Before

Changes Made

Besides increasing the height of the modal window, design recommendations would be presented first, formatted as a list of questions on one side, and answers on the other side. Eventually, a scrollbar would be integrated within the modal window to afford the person to scroll down for viewing the materials.

After

Final Design

3D-printing just had simpler ordering. Within the company's website, Formfactories aims to digitize 3D-printing orders for customers to get their parts.

Upload files and get an estimated quote.

There are default settings for people less familiar with 3D printing, but others have the option to customize different aspects of the 3D printed part and even include a service to ensure the part is properly cleaned and ready.

Verify and editng order information.

From choosing a preferred printer to payment and delivery information, the person can check if their order is correct while being able to go back and make changes.

Track current and previous order histories.

People can view what they have ordered, where it was shipped to, and when they purchased it so that repeat purchases are easier to recall.

Compare different printers and their capabilities.

People can easily scan through and compare specifications for the FDM and SLA printers that Formfactories has to offer by having different categories on the left and descriptions side-by-side that are underneath the types of printers named (e.g. FDM and SLA).

Compare 3D printing materials and recommendations.

People can also look through the different types of materials available for both printers offers, and even gain recommendations for which materials to use.

Get answers about what Formfactories is.

For further inquiries of Formfactories’ services, a FAQ page is set up to have people get the answers they need quickly about Formfactories’ business capabilities.

Results and Reflection

The current status of the implementation of our design remains uncertain as the actual Formfactories do not currently reflect redesign efforts. However, our client also owns his partner company, Incept 3D, and has been able to incorporate best practices from the competitive analysis research that we have given him. Aspects of our website do appear to be incorporated in Incept 3D's current website.

Reflection

Human-centered systems design, as Don Norman currently puts it, really necessitates active listening to important stakeholders. This not only includes the client(s), but also team members who have different personal opinions and priorities about how to proceed with a project. From many of the failures that have resided in the implementation of the website, I understood better on the importance of work ethics, time management, the ability to decide and execute deliberate design decisions within real-world constraints, and social dynamics within a team. The output of the website redesign had its visual design equivalent to a fully-functional website, and even though more must be done to include more interactions and links, I could not have been more proud of my team to help produce something that no individual could have accomplished for the client.

Improvements

If there was more time for the project, here is how I would have proceeded.

Greater Client and Company Staff Engagement

I would have strategized a way to proceed making the rest of the project more inclusive to the staff members that work under our client and his companies (Formfactories and Incept 3D) and figure out how to best engage with them with our current research findings. This would incorporate more participatory design by having more people related to the company explain their perspectives about 3D printing and how the company's customers engage with their services.

Executing Methods to Better Understand People's Experiences to 3D Printing

The redesign of the website entailed an understanding of how to connect needs uncovered from research into potential features and experiences within the website. I would have explored more in-depth with other research methods that would have helped me uncover insights of how people evaluate a 3D-printing service, how do people normally go about choosing a 3D-printing service when they need to, and how do they use information that they have about 3D-printing to make informed decisions about outsourcing their 3D-printed parts. Rapid ideation on user flows and rapid prototyping could effectively bridge the gap between what the design features are and how these features are better informed by research and effectively translate into a desireable experience of outsourcing 3D printed parts.

Seamless Translation from High-Fidelity Mockup to Implementation

Implementation of good design practices for the client to adhere to for the execution of the website is a completely different task that would have led to real-world impact. Being able to communicate our team's insights to the client and other people involved with Formfactories' website development would empower them to make smarter, more actionable decisions on how to replicate our knowledge of making the website work for them. Being able to visit the client again, with a prepared deliverable (such as a guide) to give to the client, would be one of the many ways that the translation to website implementation would have been possible.
