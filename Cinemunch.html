Overview

This was a student group project related to product design for a college prototyping course.

To create an interactive kiosk that is informed by the problems and opportunities presented from a design brief and user research.

6 weeks

Design Challenge

Project Background

The college course, called Prototyping, has taught students the fundamentals of ideation, typography, and, of course, prototyping in design. Within the scope of the project, our group was tasked to incorporate a combination of user research, typography, visual design, and rapid ideation/prototyping to create a kiosk that is interactive, intelligent, novel, and informed by research findings. A topic that our group wanted to address was the movie going experience prior to walking through theater doors.

Constraints Faced

The kiosk had to be mindful of existing digital technologies coupled with physical components since the kiosk must be a physically tangible, real-life representation of a potential product.

As the project was part of a college course, there were set deliverables outlined in the assignment page. Thus, it was important to ground our project towards expected timelines and deliverables within the Prototyping course.

Our team, as a whole, had limited working knowledge of constructing physical objects and visual design, especially when it came to connecting our user research and design decisions to the interface and formfactor of the kiosk.

Solution

Our team made Cinemunch, an immersive, touch-based kiosk that features both ticket and food purchasing to expedite the ticketing and food ordering process for moviegoers.

Interviews

There were about 4 people that we interviewed and the questions that the team asked were revolved around their habits in preparing themselves to go to the movies. We also wanted to understand people's mental models of ordering tickets and their perspectives about food to movie theaters. These were findings and commons trends found from the people we interviewed.

Discovered Trends

Two people mentioned that they usually skip the trailers and get there when the actual movie starts, and two others said they usually arrive around 5-10 minutes into the trailers.

The movie-going experience is very likely a social experience. However, addressing hunger when going to the movies can vary from each individual as people's prior experiences from concession stands, awareness of how expensive food at the movie theaters is, and other people around them can influence people's choices on food purchases.

The concession stands expensive prices and the long lines are deterrants to purchasing foods at the movie theaters.

Insights

  • The kiosk needs to demonstrate a streamlined ordering process for tickets and food to alleviate the concern for long lines and people potentially entering the theatre after their movie times start.
  • Although the kiosk must essentially be of reasonable human scale, ordering tickets and/or food within the theater should be an experience with the least amount of friction as possible. This would be achieved by having a simple, efficient, and intuitive ordering process.
  • Some mentioned that they have never used a kiosk at the theaters because it’s either not obvious or because they the kiosks take longer to navigate than it is to order from a person behind the counters.

Findings and Insights

After initial expert reviews from our mentors as well as two user tests on the paper prototype, some of the main concerns were the context of the delivery method, as it may be discouraging and distracting for people nearby trying to watch the movie while the food would be delivered. This made us rethink about how the delivery method would work in relation to the movie theater environment that the person experiences. One person from user testing did mention about the risk of the food being stolen if they were not present at the movie theater, which would inform future interaction decisions that the delivery method would remain at the person's seat.

However, because the user testing participants explained how they were satisfied with existing aspects of ordering tickets from a cashier, it was an important reminder to combine these findings along with our competitive analysis notes on ticketing cashiers and concession stands to make the ordering process for tickets and food as a viable alternative (not an overpowering substititute) to existing services at movie theater.

Initial Visual Style Guide

I worked with my team to come up with an initial moodboard that best represented how the movie experience could be viscerally described. The movies seem to be one form of entertainment that charismatically fosters a sense of escapism, from the typical habits of everyday life into a pleasant, yet adventurous experience. The images and logos that were initially inspired would viscerally represent experiences that were immersive, pleasant, and adventurous. Because people from our interviews mentioned that they go to the movie theater with friends, I helped to explore other images and logos with other color schemes that seemed to also represent social, safe, and organized experiences.

Progression of the Physical Kiosk

Sally U single-handed managed the construction of the kiosk, so she helped to make the kiosk cleaner and add shaped physical parts that were made specifically for card swiping, cash insertion, and placement of a phone for QR code scanner. More Feedback from Mentors

During a final critique session with other teaching assistants within the course, there were several concerns related to the visual aesthetics and use of digital interface components. The lack of direction and consistency of our visual design to our current iteration of the kiosk didn't demonstrate improvements made to its physical components, so there was a need to really move forward with increasing the fidelity of our kiosk's visual aesthetics and physical components within the remaining time that our group had for the project.

Furthermore, the teaching assistants expressed confusion about the text and buttons shown during the food delivery method, which meant that the current representation of available food delivery options on the kiosk interface did not adequately address the context of people's concerns of food delivery potentially being obtrusive to the movie experience. The interface for the food delivery options doesn't not clearly emphasize recommended food delivery options when the person has arrived relative to their selected movie showtime.

Design Considerations

Many different factors associated with the kiosk were classified and defined, combining insights found from user research, user testing, TA feedback, context of use within a movie theater, and technological considerations. Some of these factors were also meant to explicitly mention again some of the goals and intended features that the kiosk would incorporate. This was done so that a clearer establishment of what the kiosk should incorporate is outlined and better reflects on the context of use of the kiosk and feedback received.

Below is the design criteria to explain what the kiosk should have.

Purpose

The kiosk functions by allowing customers to purchase or pick-up tickets as well as order food in the same transaction. They can choose to have their food picked up or delivered to their reserved seat. After purchase, the kiosk will dispense the ticket and a buzzer for orders being prepared.

Concerns are mainly with customer coherency due to one kiosk providing multiple services. The whole process should be designed to be quick enough to avoid line congestion.

Digital Screen

The UI / UX should provide multiple avenues for receiving tickets, e.g., purchase or pickup, and a scanner to redeem pre-ordered tickets and coupons. This is to be achieved through a linear touch navigation that is understood by the general moviegoing demographic.

Potential failures in the form of hardware and software errors may impede customer satisfaction. To alleviate this, the kiosk can provide a “call for help” button with a speaker system between the user and an employee.

Visual Aesthetics

The look of the kiosk should attract the moviegoers’ attention and be unintimidating by following the theater’s own provided visual style (e.g., AMC will have kiosks following their black-red-white color palette).

Multiple kiosks will be placed near the entrance with slow blinking LED lights to signal that the kiosk is available for use. During use, the LED lights can turn off or fade.

Diversity of audience opinions on what looks “good” or “bad” is inevitable. LED lights may intimidate some customers but attract others.

Storage

The kiosk should fit several rolls of ticket-printing paper to withstand at least a day’s worth of sales and must include enough space for several dispensable buzzers which could be easily reinserted by employees after use.

Despite this, it shouldn’t take up too much space so that the kiosk is overwhelmingly large. Reloadability issues require constant employee maintenance and would probably require an interface to show how much is left.

Consider providing trays or cupholders to be dispensed and taken into the theater.

Multisensory Interactions

Users should be able to touch the screen and receive physical components such as tickets and buzzers. Visual components should communicate a linear pathway for ordering tickets and food. Audio sound effects should be played to signal checkpoints and to engage the user with a little fun.

With a touch screen, tactile feedback could be limited if hardware isn’t as advanced. Also, more components means more likelihood of needing to be fixed.

Preferably, a UI that provides tactile feedback so that the user has fun pressing buttons and is ensured that their interaction went through.

Buzzer Feature

Buzzers should enable an interconnected environment that will allow the customer to take a buzzer with them into their theater screening and return it to an employee in exchange for their food order.

However, buzzers may vibrate too loudly and be distracting for other moviegoers.

Theater Employees

Theater employees should be able to facilitate interactions with the kiosks in that they maintain their storage, as well as receive buzzers from moviegoers and reinsert them into the kiosk.

Connecting Design Considerations to Features of the Physical Kiosk

Within the limited time and construction capabilities that our team members had, I did my best to lay out additional labels and physical components to include for the kiosk. This included labels for cash insertion slots, card swiping, ticket dispensing slots, and storage pockets to dispense tickets and a phone-sized buzzer.

Redesign of the Kiosk's Branding

With insights from additional feedback given from our teaching assistants as well as takeaways of the strengths and weaknesses of Ludi's attempt in the second iteration of the kiosk interface, I went back to making another moodboard and visual style guide from scratch. For the modoboard, I set constraints to pick images that emphasized on experiences that were immersive and fulfilling with worry-free environments. There were less images used with more limiting colors, which informed the use of only three colors within the new style guide.

Results and Reflection

The kiosk and the poster were both presented in a class final symposium. Classmates and other people were able to visit our kiosk and try it out. I helped to present my work along with another teammate to our mentors using the prsentation slide deck.

Reflection

This project really pushed my boundaries in translating good visual and interaction design practices into a kiosk interface that is "aware" of the physical components related to the kiosk. I especially learned how branding of a product would segway into other medium and artifacts for communication, such as a poster. Within working with my team members, we all struggled with keeping up with the multitude of projects to ensure that the final pieces of branding Cinemunch came together. But, in the end, all of us relied on active communication, trust within our roles, and taking on project initiatives to beautifully craft an experience that people liked to approach and try out. What kept me motivated to finish this project when the going got tough was a strong personal interest in going out of my comfort zone in research and learning my abilities to create a solution (and other deliverables) that tied to continuous and iterative feedback.

Improvements

Here were other things that I wanted to learn and do should there be more available time and energy to continue the project.

Exploring other questions related to the stakeholders and business of the movie industry.

The desirability of the kiosk remained in question for businesses that may or may not have building environments that support or deter the placement of kiosks. With additional time, a more robust research approach would entail observations of the existing theater spaces and interviewing both staff and visitors from movie theater companies. Topics that would cover these endeavors would be how people would evaluate their current movie going experiences, how digital technology contributes to their process of movie going, and how businesses currently address issues that people face with the experiences of obtaining tickets and food.

Gathering more inspirations from existing interfaces and physical kiosks that also have accessibility in mind.

Comparing different kiosks that are usable to as many different people as possible with or without digital types of interfaces would ensure best practices are implemented to the implementation of the physical kiosk while better understanding the user tasks that kiosk interfaces take into account.
