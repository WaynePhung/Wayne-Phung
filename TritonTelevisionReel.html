Overview

Project Type

This was an independent video editing project for Triton Television (TTV).

Objective

To update TTV's existing 2015 reel and represent the diverse pool of student talent in a medium that TTV knows best, through video.

Duration

2 months

My Role

I managed all of the video assets that was gathered and received from other people within TTV. I independently edited all of the drafts and made changes to each iteration.

Tools

Google Drive, Premiere Pro.

Affiliations

Triton Television (TTV)

Video Challenge

Background

Triton Television is a student-led organization at UC San Diego. It has and remains a hub for student filmmakers to learn and apply their craft for photography, cinematography, creative writing, acting, sound, editing and many other skills. Over the years, many video projects have been showcased during weekly General Body Meetings (GBMs) that TTV hosts and plenty of projects get handed off to TTV. The reel has served as a way to introduce student talent in film and media production for those unfamiliar to TTV and persuade prospective students to join.

Task

The reel had not been updated for the past 4 years, which was not reflective of the current student projects and coverage that TTV was involved in. The reel must be short enough for people to understand the work that TTV has created, but incorporate quite a range of footage that represents what TTV currently does.

Constraints Faced

The reel must have quality footage.
It would be impossible to include all of the hours of student films, event coverage, and other projects made into a short reel. Because the reel would be incorporated in more public settings and mediums, such as YouTube and ad-hoc screenings, there was a certain subjective standard and quality that the chosen footage should have since the reel is synonymous with TTV's reputation as a student film and media production organization. This made it difficult to decide which footage to showcase in the reel and which footage to exclude.

The reel needed to best represent the wide range of projects and settings captured.
TTV does more than just make student films. Members of TTV have volunteered for event coverage related to on-campus events, directed different school projects for classes, and even captured amazing moments from concerts. Chosen footage would have to communicate these different settings very quickly for people to understand the footage.

Project Process

Understanding the Project

I met with a couple of admin members within TTV to discuss inquiries and considerations for the reel as well as to understand what it meant to create the reel. They mentioned the importance of not just the brevity of the length of the video, but to include only a widely diverse set of the highest quality footage within the reel. I understood the stakes that justified the certain level of quality the reel needed to maintain for TTV's branding and marketing reasons, and I sought after inspiration on YouTube for existing film reels.

Getting and Managing Media Files

Over the course of several months, I sent out multiple reminders to a public TTV Slack channel as well as individual private messages for people to upload their work into a Google Drive folder dedicated to storing footage for the reel. This ensured secure storage and management of files by having a digital back up of footage, which was sorted by folders named after each individual's submitted work. A similar folder structure would be replicated in Premiere Pro (a video editor program by Adobe).

Finding and Remixing a Soundtrack

Then, I decided to find a soundtrack that was suitable for the reel. My preference was to find a soundtrack that had tempo changes, some energy in its beat, a bit of suspense, and plenty of layering of different tunes and beats. Finding a Creative Commons license song on YouTube that had a compelling, dynamic music track was not an easy endeavor, but I eventually found this song to be a great fit for the video, Interstellar Travel by Mark Tyner. I remixed the soundtrack to make it fit within slightly over 2 minutes for the reel. Feel free to hear the initial remixed version.

Editing and Choosing Footage

With hours of footage organized and assembled on Premiere Pro and with the soundtrack chosen, I glanced through the video footage and selected those that have the potential to best showcase the reel. There were some sections of the reel that incorporated a series of related parts for specific video works chosen. I allowed the initial process to let myself explore and experiment with the footage gathered. Although some of the clips seemed to fit really well with some of the parts of the soundtrack, I was not too committed with other clips that may not showcase strong cinematography (clips that had interesting, dynamic framing/movement of objects and camera views), or that didn't contribute to the spectrum of projects that TTV covered (event coverage, film festival works, etc.).

Examples of Initial Clips Considered

This was a series of clips from Cole Takayama's short film "A Beautiful View" that was largely considered as the opening of the reel. These were initially considered because of how well the shots framed the characters and camera movements between the clips while having outside environments that were uniquely different from other student works. However, it remained at large as to how well it can best represent TTV's branding and craft for film and media production.

Another series of clips that I felt more committed to was from Trae Whyte's film "Koulan". The close-up camera views of the dancers' facial expressions and their dynamic dance movements made the clips incredibly simple for anyone to interpret what the clips conveyed in a short amount of time. The types of clips from this film that would best represent TTV's potential body of work and skills were promising, but yet to be properly ordered to largely contribute to the storytelling of the reel.

These clips were taken from different on-campus events and were more geared towards festivals and concerts. Although the clips were rich with visual details to give the viewer enough context of what events and concert coverage look like, they seem to be busy with lots of people, lights, and objects happening and a cohesive narrative would be lost. Some of the shots would be carefully chosen and excluded for future drafts and further experimentation of the order of the clips.

Visual Effects

From prior inspiration of film reels found online, most contained little to no added effects to the original footage. This meant that the footage should remain the primary focus, with minimal amount of edits included to enhance the storytelling or flow of the reel. This was also an exploratory phase to not just experiment with the footage itself but to determine how transitions and effects crucialy contribute to the reel. First Draft

The first draft had more sections of related footage from the same works. But with other sections that had more random footage strewn together, there was something (or some things) missing within this draft to tell an effective story of what TTV is. Nevertheless, this draft enabled me to truly understand first-hand of how to paint a picture of student work within TTV.

Evaluation and Feedback

I showed the reel to three of the student executive members within TTV as well as around two other random TTV members who happened to be present when I finished making the draft. Besides the need to eventually incorporate color correction and grading for stylizing the footage, changes were needed to create a stronger, more interesting narrative of the reel. One person specifically shared his opinions of which shots were good, okay, or not good enough (many were not good enough) while another person helped to clarify some of the things to look for that qualifies video clips to be included in the reel. It was a reminder for me to show only the best clips that best contribute to the narrative the reel should capture, and to remain flexible in experimenting with other clips.

Furthermore, there were some issues with the timing of the clips to certain moments of the soundtrack. some of these clips didn’t have content that was different enough to contribute within the narrative and the diverse pool of clips that should be represented in the reel. Some of my other peers also voiced that particular video clips were either not persuasive or compelling enough to want them to view more of what the reel has to offer. This meant not just creative an overarching narrative for the reel, but to be more critical of the individual contributions that each clip had to the narrative.

Iterating

Future drafts captured some more creative uses of some of the edits by adding more sections dedicated to concerts and events and leveraging sequences of clips to have related movement from each other. From another person's feedback, there were a lot of faces staring into the camera and just a lot of medium shots of people, so I had to continuously assess if some of the shots were too fast to be recognized or if there were other shots that could improve the diversity of the clips to tell a more compelling story. Admittedly, some of my choices for some of the clips were more biased towards key moments towards the music, but if the clips themselves seemed to evoke the right kinds of moments while still connecting well to other clips, they would be fine to keep.

The following clips are examples of changes made from different drafts.

Movement and Cinematography

A visible, seemingly logical connection between the spatial locations and movement of objects within the cinematography of different clips would strongly develop that narrative of TTV having high-caliber student projects that successfully execute different camera shots and different foreground/background movement of subjects.

More Related Grouping of Shots

Diversity of clips can be represented, even if the framing of the shots are similar, as long as there are notable differences in lighting, facial expressions, and movements (from subjects of the shot or from the camera). In the examples below, this would be used towards the end of the reel.

A Subtler, but More Meaningful Ending

Earlier drafts had lots of film grunge overlays during the climatic beat drop of the soundtrack and towards the end. Keeping the tone subtle helps to achieve a stylistic, grunge look while letting the footage speak for itself and TTV's potential for achieving quality cinematic work.

Final Draft

Results

The reel is up on YouTube to view. It is currently the featured video on TTV's YouTube channel, which will help introduce people to the work that TTV does as a student film and media production organization.

Reflection

This was one of the earliest student organizations at University of California - San Diego that I have joined, and it inspired me to not just learn media and filmmaking, but it empowered me to go outside of my comfort zones to learn about filmmaking lingo, post-production software like Premiere Pro, and appreciate the power of storytelling and an excellent narrative for people to be inspired by. I would not have accomplished any of my future video projects without the guidance and support from my peers and student executive members at the organization, and I wanted this project to be a lasting legacy, an ode to the projects that were never revealed to the general public. The video would be dedicated to alumni who were students just like existing TTV members, to existing student members who want to remain involved and motivated to do good filmmmaking, and to prospective students who wanted to understand filmmaking from TTV members/students on campus. Looking back, I still remain hungry to continue learning more about post-production and other aspects of video making for the years to come.

That's concludes the media project! Thank you. I really do appreciate you taking a bit of your time reading what I have produced and learned. I only hope that you have learned something new from this project.
